Phishing Emails Generated by ChatGPT & Human Models Gained 81% Click Rates

A landmark cybersecurity study reveals that AI-generated phishing emails are now as effective as those crafted by expert human social engineers — and far cheaper to produce at scale.

Uncategorized
August 7, 2023 7 min read ARG Editorial Team

A landmark study comparing AI-generated phishing emails with those crafted by experienced human social engineers has found that both achieved click-through rates as high as 81% — a finding that has sent shockwaves through the global cybersecurity community. The research, which tested multiple large language models including ChatGPT against a panel of human participants across a range of professional and demographic backgrounds, reveals that the gap between human and machine-generated deception has effectively closed — with profound implications for how organisations defend against email-based attacks.

What the Research Found

The study, conducted by a team of cybersecurity and behavioural science researchers, presented participants with a series of emails — some crafted by expert human social engineers using established phishing techniques, others generated by large language models given detailed instructions about the target's profile, role, and likely concerns. Participants were asked to interact with their email as they normally would during a working day.

The results were stark. Both the human-crafted and AI-generated emails achieved click rates in the 75–81% range under conditions that simulated realistic workplace email environments. When personalisation data was incorporated — pulling publicly available information about the target from LinkedIn, company websites, and social media — click rates for AI-generated emails rose to match or exceed those achieved by experienced human attackers.

"The assumption that AI-generated content is somehow easier to spot than human-written content is already false — and it is going to become more false every month as these models improve."

Why This Matters

The implications of this finding extend well beyond academic interest. Until now, one limiting factor on the scale of sophisticated spear-phishing attacks has been the labour required to craft convincing, personalised emails. An experienced human social engineer can research and write perhaps 10 to 20 high-quality targeted phishing emails per day. With AI assistance, the same attacker can generate thousands — each personalised, contextually plausible, and grammatically flawless — in the same time period.

This represents a fundamental change in the economics of phishing. Attacks that previously required significant expertise and time investment can now be conducted at industrial scale by attackers with minimal technical skill. The barrier to entry for sophisticated social engineering has effectively collapsed.

The Human Vulnerability Factor

What the study also confirmed — and what cybersecurity researchers have argued for years — is that the primary vulnerability exploited by phishing attacks is not technical but psychological. The most effective phishing emails, whether human or AI-generated, succeed by exploiting a small set of cognitive biases and emotional responses: urgency, authority, fear, curiosity, and social proof.

AI language models are, in a meaningful sense, expert students of human psychology. Trained on vast quantities of human-written text, they have absorbed patterns of persuasion, emotional appeal, and social manipulation with a fidelity that rivals the most skilled human communicators. When given a specific target profile and an objective, they can construct messages that hit precisely the psychological triggers most likely to produce the desired action.

What Organisations Must Do Differently

The findings make clear that traditional security awareness training — which typically teaches employees to look for poor grammar, suspicious sender addresses, and generic content — is no longer adequate as a primary defence against AI-assisted phishing. Organisations must fundamentally rethink their approach:

  • Move from rule-based to behavioural training — instead of teaching employees a checklist of technical indicators to spot, train them on the psychological mechanisms attackers exploit, so they can recognise manipulation attempts regardless of surface appearance.
  • Conduct continuous simulation, not annual training — the muscle memory required to resist sophisticated phishing develops through repeated, realistic practice, not a once-a-year e-learning module.
  • Implement technical controls that reduce reliance on human judgement — email authentication standards, link sandboxing, and attachment scanning cannot stop all attacks, but they reduce the volume that reaches employees in the first place.
  • Establish clear verification procedures for high-risk requests — any request involving financial transfers, credential changes, or sensitive data access should require out-of-band verification through a known, trusted channel, regardless of how convincing the email appears.
  • Assume compromise and monitor accordingly — given the effectiveness of AI-assisted phishing, organisations should assume that some percentage of employees will be successfully deceived and invest in detection and response capabilities rather than purely prevention.

The Road Ahead

The research team noted that the large language models used in the study represent the technology as it existed at time of testing — and that the models available to attackers today are substantially more capable than those available a year ago. The trajectory of improvement in AI language generation shows no sign of slowing.

This means that the 81% click rate achieved in controlled conditions today may represent a floor rather than a ceiling. As models become more capable of gathering and processing open-source intelligence about targets, and as voice and video synthesis technologies mature alongside text generation, the attack surface for AI-assisted social engineering will continue to expand.

Organisations that wait for the threat to stabilise before adapting their defences are, security researchers warn, making a very costly miscalculation.
