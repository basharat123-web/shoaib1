Big and Small Companies on the Radar of Data Hackers

What are the chances of your personal data being hacked? According to cybersecurity experts, the answer is alarming — and no business is too small to be a target.

Uncategorized
November 14, 2023 5 min read ARG Editorial Team

BUFFALO, N.Y. (WIVB) — What are the chances of your personal data being hacked? "It's very high. It's probably already out there," says cybersecurity expert Arun Vishwanath, author of The Weakest Link and a leading researcher in human vulnerability to cyberattacks. Whether you run a Fortune 500 corporation or a small neighbourhood business, hackers are actively targeting your data — and the misconception that only large enterprises are at risk leaves millions of smaller organisations dangerously exposed.

The Myth of the Small-Business Safe Zone

One of the most persistent and dangerous myths in cybersecurity is that small businesses are not attractive targets for hackers. The reality is precisely the opposite. Small businesses are increasingly prime targets because they typically have weaker security infrastructure, less-trained staff, and fewer resources dedicated to monitoring and incident response.

"Hackers don't discriminate based on company size," Vishwanath explained. "They look for the easiest entry point. And small businesses, which often think they have nothing worth stealing, are often the easiest doors to open."

According to the FBI's Internet Crime Report, cybercrime losses in the United States reached over $10 billion in 2022 alone — a record high — with small and medium-sized businesses accounting for a rapidly growing share of victims.

What Hackers Are After

Whether targeting a large corporation or a local business, hackers are primarily after three things: financial credentials, customer personal information, and login data that can be used to pivot into connected systems. Ransomware attacks — in which hackers encrypt a company's data and demand payment for its release — have surged dramatically in recent years. Small businesses are particularly vulnerable because they often lack the resources to restore systems from backup or engage specialists to recover their data.

"The human element is always the weakest link. Hackers know this, and they exploit it relentlessly."

How Hackers Get In

The vast majority of successful breaches begin not with sophisticated technical exploits, but with simple human error. Phishing emails that trick an employee into clicking a malicious link or entering credentials on a fake website are responsible for the majority of initial access events across both large and small organisations. Social engineering — manipulating people through deception rather than hacking systems — remains the preferred entry point for cybercriminals worldwide.

Other common entry points include unpatched software with known vulnerabilities, weak or reused passwords, and inadequately secured remote access tools — particularly relevant for organisations that shifted to remote work during the pandemic and never fully addressed the security gaps that created.

Protecting Your Business: Practical Steps

Cybersecurity experts recommend the following steps for organisations of all sizes:

  • Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all accounts, especially email, financial platforms, and remote access tools.
  • Train employees regularly with realistic phishing simulations and social engineering awareness sessions — not just annual compliance tick-box exercises.
  • Patch and update all software promptly — a significant proportion of successful breaches exploit known vulnerabilities that had available fixes.
  • Back up data to offline or air-gapped systems that cannot be reached through the internet, ensuring recovery is possible without paying a ransom.
  • Develop an incident response plan so your team knows exactly what to do — and who to call — in the first hours after a breach is discovered.

The Bigger Picture

The cybersecurity threat landscape has grown dramatically more complex. Nation-state actors, organised criminal groups, and opportunistic individual hackers all operate in the same ecosystem — each with different motivations but equally capable of causing devastating financial and reputational damage.

For consumers, every breach at a company you have ever done business with potentially exposes your personal information to bad actors. Name, address, email address, phone number, financial details — all of it can be purchased on dark web marketplaces for a matter of dollars.

"Assume your data is already out there," Vishwanath advises. "The question is not whether you have been affected by a breach — it is what you are doing right now to protect yourself from what comes next."
