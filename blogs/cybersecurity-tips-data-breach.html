Cybersecurity Expert Shares Tips to Protect Yourself in Wake of Concerns Over Massive Data Breach

The data is already out there. Cybersecurity expert Arun Vishwanath explains exactly what to do — and how quickly you need to act.

Uncategorized
September 12, 2024 6 min read ARG Editorial Team

BUFFALO, N.Y. (WKBW/ABC) — Following the discovery of one of the largest data breaches in recorded history — a database containing more than 26 billion stolen records from thousands of organisations worldwide — leading cybersecurity expert Arun Vishwanath is urging consumers and businesses alike to take immediate protective action. "The data is already out there," Vishwanath says bluntly. "The question is not whether you have been affected. The question is how quickly you act before someone else does."

Why You Need to Act Now, Not Later

In the hours and days following a major breach disclosure, attackers move fast. Criminal groups that maintain access to large aggregated databases use them to launch credential stuffing attacks — systematically testing leaked username and password combinations across hundreds of popular platforms simultaneously. If you reuse passwords across multiple accounts, even one account appearing in a breach database can result in a cascade of compromises across your entire digital life.

Vishwanath notes that the window between a breach being publicly disclosed and attackers launching exploitation campaigns based on the leaked data is measured in hours, not days. "Most people think they have time to deal with this. They do not. Every hour you wait is an hour an attacker may have already used."

"Do not wait for confirmation that your specific account was compromised. Assume it was, and act accordingly. The cost of acting unnecessarily is an hour of your time. The cost of not acting could be your savings, your identity, and years of your life."

Step One: Change Your Passwords — Strategically

The first and most urgent step is changing passwords — but doing so strategically rather than randomly. Vishwanath recommends prioritising accounts in the following order:

  • Your primary email account — this is the master key to your digital identity. Whoever controls your email can reset the password on virtually every other account you own.
  • Financial accounts — banking, investment platforms, payment services such as PayPal and Venmo, and cryptocurrency wallets.
  • Healthcare portals — medical records and health insurance accounts contain sensitive personal information that is highly valuable to identity thieves.
  • Social media accounts — compromised social media accounts are used to run scams targeting your contacts, damage your reputation, and gather further personal intelligence.
  • All other accounts — work systematically through every service you use, ensuring each has a unique, randomly generated password.

Step Two: Enable Multi-Factor Authentication Everywhere

Multi-factor authentication (MFA) is the single most effective security control available to ordinary users. When MFA is enabled, an attacker who obtains your password still cannot access your account without also compromising your second factor — typically a code sent to your phone or generated by an authenticator app.

Vishwanath strongly recommends using an authenticator app (such as Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, or Authy) rather than SMS-based codes wherever possible, as SIM-swapping attacks can allow criminals to intercept text messages sent to your phone number. "Authenticator apps are not perfect, but they are substantially more secure than SMS codes and dramatically better than nothing," he notes.

Step Three: Use a Password Manager

The single reason most people reuse passwords — the behaviour that makes large-scale credential stuffing attacks so devastatingly effective — is that remembering dozens of unique complex passwords is genuinely impossible without assistance. Password managers solve this problem by securely storing randomly generated unique passwords for every account, requiring only one strong master password to unlock them.

Reputable password managers including Bitwarden (free and open source), 1Password, and Dashlane use strong encryption that means even if the password manager company itself is breached, your stored passwords cannot be accessed. "Using a password manager is not a luxury," Vishwanath says. "In 2024, it is basic digital hygiene."

Step Four: Freeze Your Credit

If your personal information — name, address, date of birth, and Social Security number — has been exposed in a breach, identity thieves may attempt to open fraudulent accounts in your name using that information. A credit freeze prevents any new credit accounts from being opened without your explicit authorisation, regardless of what personal information an attacker possesses.

Credit freezes are free, can be placed and lifted online in minutes, and do not affect your existing accounts or credit score. Vishwanath recommends placing freezes with all three major credit bureaus simultaneously, as each maintains a separate database.

Step Five: Raise Your Vigilance Against Phishing

In the weeks following a major breach disclosure, attackers consistently launch elevated volumes of phishing campaigns targeting affected users — often impersonating the breached organisation, banks, or government agencies, and using leaked data to make their messages appear credible and personalised.

Be especially sceptical of any unsolicited communication — email, text message, or phone call — that references a recent breach, asks you to verify your account, or creates a sense of urgency around taking an immediate action. When in doubt, navigate directly to the organisation's official website by typing the address in your browser rather than clicking any link in a message.

The Longer-Term Mindset Shift

Beyond the immediate protective steps, Vishwanath advocates for a fundamental shift in how ordinary people think about their digital security — moving from a reactive posture, in which they respond to breaches after they happen, to a proactive one in which good security habits are maintained continuously.

"We have to stop thinking of cybersecurity as something you deal with when there is a problem," he says. "Your front door has a lock on it not because your house has already been broken into, but because you understand it is a dangerous world. Your digital life needs the same ongoing attention."
