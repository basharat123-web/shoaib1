Stopping Russian Cyberattacks at Their Source

Step up training, teach social engineering awareness, share monitoring tools — and shift the strategy from reactive defence to proactive disruption.

Uncategorized
March 18, 2022 7 min read ARG Editorial Team

As Russian state-sponsored cyber operations continue to escalate against Western governments, critical infrastructure operators, and private sector organisations, cybersecurity experts are calling for a fundamental shift in strategy — moving from reactive damage control to proactive disruption of attacks before they reach their targets. "We keep playing whack-a-mole," says cybersecurity researcher Arun Vishwanath, whose work on human vulnerability has been cited by NATO, the NSA, and the World Economic Forum. "Every time we patch one hole, they find another. We need to change the nature of the game."

The Scale of the Threat

Russian state-sponsored cyber operations have grown substantially in both sophistication and ambition over the past decade. From large-scale supply chain attacks compromising government and corporate networks to destructive malware causing billions of dollars in global damage, Russian threat actors have demonstrated both the technical capability and the strategic willingness to conduct attacks at a scale previously associated only with kinetic military operations.

The conflict in Ukraine has further accelerated this trend, with cyber warfare integrated directly into military campaign planning. Attacks on power infrastructure, communications networks, and financial systems have been timed to coincide with ground offensives — offering a blueprint that adversaries worldwide are studying closely.

Why Current Defences Are Falling Short

The dominant model of cybersecurity defence — patch vulnerabilities, update antivirus software, deploy firewalls — was designed for a threat landscape that no longer exists. Russian state-sponsored actors do not rely primarily on known software exploits. They rely on human beings. Social engineering, spear-phishing, and credential theft through deception account for the majority of successful initial access events, regardless of how well-patched an organisation's systems are.

"The technical perimeter is a useful thing to defend, but it is not where most attacks succeed," Vishwanath explains. "Most attacks succeed because someone clicked something they should not have clicked, or trusted someone they should not have trusted. Technology cannot fully fix a human problem."

Five Strategies for Stopping Attacks at Their Source

1. Step Up Training with Realistic Drills

Annual security awareness training delivered through compliance e-learning platforms is largely ineffective against sophisticated phishing campaigns. Organisations need to conduct regular, scenario-based phishing simulations that mirror current attack techniques — including AI-generated emails, voice cloning, and SMS-based smishing attacks. Employees who experience realistic simulated attacks dramatically reduce their real-world click rates.

2. Teach Social Engineering Awareness in Depth

Russian intelligence services are highly skilled at human manipulation — deploying fake personas across social media, exploiting emotional triggers such as urgency, fear, and authority, and building long-term trust before executing an attack. Employees need detailed, specific education about these tactics, not just generic advice to "think before you click."

3. Share Open Source Monitoring Tools and Threat Intelligence

Threat intelligence sharing between government agencies, industry sector groups, and international partners allows defenders to identify indicators of compromise before an active attack reaches its target. Many effective monitoring tools are freely available through open source communities — organisations that are not using them are leaving significant defensive capability on the table.

4. Implement Zero-Trust Architecture

Zero-trust security models operate on the assumption that every user, device, and network connection may already be compromised. Rather than trusting users once they have authenticated inside the perimeter, zero-trust systems require continuous verification of identity and device health. This dramatically limits the damage an attacker can cause even after successfully obtaining credentials.

5. Coordinate International Diplomatic and Legal Responses

Technical defences alone are insufficient without meaningful consequences for attackers. Coordinated cyber sanctions, criminal indictments of named threat actors, and sustained diplomatic pressure across allied nations are essential components of a deterrence strategy. When state-sponsored hackers operate with complete impunity, the cost-benefit calculation strongly favours continued attacks.

"Deterrence requires that the cost of attacking exceed the benefit. Right now, for most Russian cyber operations, it does not."

The Bottom Line

Stopping Russian cyberattacks at their source requires a combination of technical measures, human-centred training, intelligence sharing, and coordinated international action. No single organisation can accomplish this alone — but every organisation can dramatically reduce its own vulnerability by investing in the human layer of its defence, which remains the most consistently exploited point of entry in virtually every successful breach.

The goal, Vishwanath argues, is not perfect security — which is unachievable — but making attacks costly enough, and difficult enough, that the adversary's calculus changes.
